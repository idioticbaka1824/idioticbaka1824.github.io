Misc

Problem statement:

We have an audio clip containing both a sound effect and background music, and another audio clip containing the corresponding background music separately.
We want to remove the background music so as to isolate the sound effect.
The obvious solution is to subtract the background audio from the combined audio, but this only works if both audios are exactly aligned, which may not be the case. The separate background audio may be slightly out of phase with the background in the combined audio, which complicates things.

Model:

Let the combined audio $f(t)$ be composed of two sounds: the sound effect $s(t)$ and the background music $b(t)$. Thus, $f(t)=s(t)+b(t)$.
Let the available, possibly shifted background audio be represented as $b_a(t)=b(t-a)$.
Let the Fourier transform of a function be defined as $\widehat{f}(\omega)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}f(t)e^{-i\omega t}\,dt$

Then, in the frequency domain, we have two equations $$\begin{align} \tag{1}\widehat{f}(\omega)&=\widehat{s}(\omega)+\widehat{b}(\omega) \\ \tag{2}\widehat{b_a}(\omega)&=e^{-ia\omega}\widehat{b}(\omega) \end{align}$$ in three unknowns $$\widehat{s}(\omega),\;\widehat{b}(\omega),\;a$$

Solution:

This appears intractable upon first glance, but recall the properties of the Fourier transform: $$\tag{3}\widehat{\frac{df(t)}{dt}}=i\omega\widehat{f}(\omega)$$ We see that taking the derivative of the audio signal in the time domain gives us some more information to work with. Thus, $$\begin{align} \tag{4}\widehat{f'(t)}(\omega)&=\widehat{s'(t)}+\widehat{b'(t)}=i\omega\widehat{f}(\omega)=i\omega\left(\widehat{s}(\omega)+\widehat{b}(\omega)\right)\\ \tag{5}\widehat{b'_a(t)}(\omega)&=i\omega\widehat{b_a}(\omega)=i\omega e^{-ia\omega}\widehat{b}(\omega) \end{align}$$ Since $f'(t)$ and $b_a'(t)$ are calculable from $f(t)$ and $b(t)$, we have gained two equations at no cost. We can now solve for $\widehat{s}(\omega)$ quite straightforwardly.

From $(4)$ we have $$\tag{6}\widehat{s}=-\frac{i}{\omega}\widehat{f'}-\widehat{b}$$ and from $(5)$ we have $$\tag{7}\widehat{b}=-\frac{i}{\omega}e^{ia\omega}\widehat{b'_a}$$ Substituting $(7)$ in $(6)$ yields $$\tag{8}\widehat{s}=\frac{i}{\omega}\left(e^{ia\omega}\widehat{b'_a}-\widehat{f'}\right)$$ Now, from $(1)$ and $(2)$ we have $$\tag{9}e^{ia\omega}=\frac{\widehat{b}}{\widehat{b_a}}=\frac{\widehat{f}-\widehat{s}}{\widehat{b_a}}$$ Substituting $(9)$ in $(8)$ yields $$\tag{10}\widehat{s}=\frac{i\widehat{b'_a}\widehat{f}}{\omega\widehat{b_a}}-\frac{i\widehat{b'_a}\widehat{s}}{\omega\widehat{b_a}}-\frac{i\widehat{f'}}{\omega}$$ Solving $(10)$ for $\widehat{s}$ finally gives us $$\tag{11}\widehat{s}(\omega)=\frac{\widehat{b'_a}\widehat{f}-\widehat{f'}}{\widehat{b'_a}-i\omega\widehat{b_a}}$$ Inverting $(11)$ returns $s(t)$, the isolated sound effect desired. $\blacksquare$

Note that this derivation uses the unitary definition of the Fourier transform in units of angular frequency, on a continuous domain. Some changes must be made when implementing this in code.
